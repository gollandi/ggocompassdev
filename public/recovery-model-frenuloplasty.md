## Procedure-Level Fields (`ggoProcedure`)

### preOperativeInstructions

You will have this procedure under local anaesthesia (an injection to numb the penis). There is no need to fast beforehand — you may eat and drink as normal on the day of surgery.

No special preparation is required the night before. On the morning of surgery:
- Shower and wash the genital area using your normal soap. There is no need for special antiseptic wash unless instructed.
- Wear loose, comfortable clothing — tracksuit bottoms or loose-fitting shorts are ideal. Avoid tight jeans or fitted underwear.
- Bring supportive underwear (snug briefs, not boxers) to wear after the procedure. This helps hold a light dressing in place and reduces movement of the wound.
- You do not need someone to drive you home — most men are comfortable to travel independently after a local anaesthetic procedure. However, you may prefer company for reassurance.
- Leave jewellery and valuables at home.

Stop any blood-thinning medication (aspirin, ibuprofen, warfarin, rivaroxaban) only if your surgeon has specifically told you to do so — do not stop these without instruction.

You will typically arrive 30–60 minutes before your procedure time. Mr Ollandini will see you beforehand to mark the site and answer any remaining questions.

### postOperativeInstructions

You will go home the same day, usually within 30–60 minutes of the procedure. The local anaesthetic wears off within 2–4 hours — take your first dose of paracetamol before the numbness fades.

Key points for the first 4 weeks:
- Wear supportive briefs (not boxers) for the first 7 days. This limits movement of the penis against clothing and protects the wound.
- Take paracetamol (1g) every 6 hours regularly for the first 2–3 days, as prescribed by your surgeon. Ibuprofen (400mg) can be added if needed and not contraindicated — always take with food.
- Keep the wound dry for 48 hours. After that, shower normally and let warm water run over the area — do not scrub. Pat dry gently with a clean towel.
- Do not soak in a bath, swim, or use a hot tub for 2 weeks.
- The sutures are dissolvable and do not need removal. They typically dissolve within 7–14 days.
- Erections during healing are expected and will not damage the repair. If an erection causes discomfort, sitting upright and applying gentle pressure with a cool flannel may help.
- Avoid sexual activity (including masturbation) for 4 weeks or until cleared by your surgeon.
- You may return to desk-based work from day 3–5. Physical or manual work may require 7–10 days off depending on comfort.

Your follow-up is by telephone or in person at 2–4 weeks, arranged at discharge.

### expectedOutcomes

Tenderness and mild swelling around the frenulum (the small band of tissue on the underside of the glans connecting the foreskin to the shaft) typically resolve within 7–10 days. The foreskin is preserved — this procedure does not remove any skin.

The frenuloplasty lengthens the frenulum using a Z-plasty or Y-V plasty technique. This permanently corrects the tightness, meaning the frenulum will not tear or restrict again after healing.

Sensitivity at the frenulum site is increased initially due to the high concentration of nerve endings in this area. This settles over 2–4 weeks and typically returns to a comfortable baseline. Some men report that sensitivity improves long-term compared to the pre-operative state where the tight frenulum caused discomfort during retraction.

The final scar is a small line on the underside of the glans-foreskin junction. It is not visible when the foreskin is in its natural position. Full scar maturation takes 3–6 months, during which the scar softens and flattens.

Frenuloplasty has a high success rate with low recurrence. Tearing or re-tightening after a properly performed plasty is uncommon (EAU 2024).

**normalExperiences**

You have had a frenuloplasty today. The procedure was performed under local anaesthesia through a small incision on the underside of the glans (the head of the penis) at the frenulum — the band of tissue connecting the foreskin to the shaft.

You may notice:
- Numbness of the penis from the local anaesthetic — this wears off within 2–4 hours
- A small dressing or adhesive strip on the underside of the glans
- Mild bleeding or blood-staining on the dressing — this is expected given the rich blood supply in this area
- Once the anaesthetic wears off, a tender, stinging sensation at the frenulum site
- Mild swelling around the frenulum and inner foreskin

**forecast**

The first 48 hours involve the most tenderness. The frenulum area is small, so swelling is localised and less dramatic than after circumcision. Most men find the discomfort manageable with regular paracetamol. By day 3, the tenderness is typically settling well.

**redFlags**

- Heavy bleeding that soaks through the dressing and does not stop with 10 minutes of firm, gentle pressure
- Severe pain not controlled by regular paracetamol and ibuprofen
- Fever above 38°C
- Foreskin stuck in a retracted position behind the glans and unable to be brought forward (paraphimosis — this is an emergency, attend A&E immediately)
- Difficulty passing urine
- Rapidly increasing swelling of the glans or foreskin

Take your first dose of paracetamol before the local anaesthetic wears off — do not wait for pain to develop. **nurseName**

Caroline

**whyThisHappens**

The frenulum has an exceptionally rich blood supply relative to its size. It is supplied by the frenular artery — a branch of the dorsal artery of the penis. This is why even small cuts or tears to the frenulum can bleed significantly, and why you may notice blood-staining on the dressing. The blood supply also means the area heals quickly, as oxygen and nutrients are delivered efficiently to the wound.

The tissue of the frenulum is thin and contains a high density of nerve endings (Meissner's corpuscles and free nerve endings). This concentration of sensory receptors is why the area feels particularly tender after surgery and why the local anaesthetic is so effective — there is a rich nerve network to block.

**nurseName**

Sarah

**whyThisHappens**

The swelling you see is localised tissue oedema — fluid that the body's immune system has directed to the surgical site as part of the healing process. Unlike circumcision, where the entire foreskin edge is a wound, frenuloplasty involves a single small incision on the underside. This means the inflammatory response is proportionally smaller: less tissue disruption, less oedema, less bruising.

The pulling sensation during erections occurs because the suture line is on the ventral (underside) surface, which stretches during tumescence. The Z-plasty or Y-V plasty technique distributes this tension across a geometrically rearranged flap rather than a straight line, which is precisely why the repair is stronger than the original frenulum.

**whyThisHappens**

The heightened sensitivity at the frenulum is because this area contains one of the highest densities of sensory nerve endings on the penis. During this regeneration phase, nerve signals can be amplified or altered, producing sensations that feel disproportionately intense. This is temporary. As the nerves complete their repair and the surrounding scar tissue matures, sensitivity returns to a comfortable baseline — typically within 2–4 weeks.

**nurseName**

Michael

**whyThisHappens**

The sutures used in frenuloplasty are made of polyglactin (Vicryl Rapide) or similar rapidly absorbable material. They dissolve through hydrolysis — water molecules break the chemical bonds in the suture polymer. This process accelerates in warm, moist environments, which is why sutures in the genital area dissolve faster than those in drier locations. The suture loses approximately 50% of its tensile strength within 5 days and is fully absorbed within 10–14 days. By the time the suture dissolves, the wound has enough of its own collagen-based strength to hold together.

**whyThisHappens**

At two weeks, the wound has transitioned from the proliferative phase into early remodelling. During the proliferative phase (days 3–14), fibroblasts deposited a dense matrix of type III collagen to bridge the wound. This collagen is disorganised — laid down rapidly for strength rather than elegance. During the remodelling phase that begins now, the body replaces type III collagen with stronger type I collagen and reorganises the fibres along the lines of tension. This is why the scar initially feels firm and then gradually softens over weeks to months. The wound currently has approximately 20–25% of its final tensile strength — adequate for daily activities but not yet ready for the friction and stretching of sexual activity.

**whyThisHappens**

At 4 weeks, the wound has approximately 30–40% of its final tensile strength — sufficient for all normal activities including sexual intercourse. The remodelling phase continues for 3–6 months, during which the scar progressively strengthens and softens.

The Z-plasty or Y-V plasty geometry is key to why this repair is permanent. A straight-line scar tends to contract along its length (scar contracture). The Z-plasty rearranges the tissue into two triangular flaps that interdigitate — effectively converting a short, tight band into a longer, more elastic one. The geometry distributes tension across multiple axes rather than a single line. This is the same plastic surgery principle used to release scar contractures throughout the body. The result is a frenulum that is permanently longer and more supple than the original tight band, with no tendency to re-tighten.
