## Procedure-Level Fields (`ggoProcedure`)

### preOperativeInstructions

You will have this procedure under general or regional anaesthesia (spinal). Follow your fasting instructions: no food for 6 hours and clear water only up to 2 hours before your scheduled arrival time.

Stop any blood-thinning medication (aspirin, ibuprofen, warfarin, rivaroxaban) only if your surgeon has specifically told you to do so — do not stop these without instruction.

On the morning of surgery:
- Shower using your normal soap. There is no need for special antiseptic wash unless instructed.
- Wear loose, comfortable clothing — preferably tracksuit bottoms or similar. Avoid tight jeans.
- Bring supportive underwear (briefs, not boxers) to wear immediately after surgery. If you do not have any, your nurse will provide scrotal support.
- Arrange for a responsible adult to collect you. You will not be able to drive yourself home.
- Leave jewellery and valuables at home.

You will typically arrive 1–2 hours before your procedure time. Your anaesthetist and Mr Ollandini will see you beforehand to answer any remaining questions.

### postOperativeInstructions

You will go home the same day once you have passed urine, eaten a light meal, and your pain is comfortable with oral medication.

Key points for the first 3 weeks:
- Wear scrotal support (firm briefs or a jockstrap) continuously for 14 days, including at night. This reduces swelling and improves comfort significantly.
- Apply a wrapped ice pack to the scrotum for 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off, for the first 48 hours. Do not apply ice directly to the skin.
- Take paracetamol (1g) every 6 hours regularly for the first 3 days, as prescribed by your surgeon. Ibuprofen (400mg) can be added if needed and not contraindicated — always take with food.
- Keep the wound dry for 48 hours. After that, shower normally and pat the area dry. Do not take baths or swim for 2 weeks.
- Avoid lifting anything heavier than a full kettle for 2 weeks. No gym, running, or contact sports for 3 weeks.
- You may return to desk-based work after 3–5 days. Physical or manual work requires 2–3 weeks off.
- Sexual activity: avoid for 2 weeks minimum. Resume only when comfortable.

Your follow-up appointment is typically at 6 weeks. If the procedure was performed for fertility, a semen analysis is arranged at 3 months post-operatively.

### expectedOutcomes

Scrotal aching and swelling typically resolve within 2–3 weeks. Bruising may track down towards the inner thigh — this looks dramatic but is harmless and reabsorbs within 10–14 days.

If the procedure was performed for pain: most men report significant improvement within 4–6 weeks. Approximately 80% of men experience complete or near-complete resolution of scrotal aching (EAU 2024).

If the procedure was performed for fertility: semen parameters (count, motility, morphology) may begin to improve from 3 months post-operatively, with peak improvement at 6–12 months. Improvement in at least one semen parameter is seen in approximately 60–70% of men (EAU 2024). Natural conception rates following varicocelectomy are approximately 30–40% within 12 months.

The microsurgical approach has the lowest complication rates of any varicocele repair technique: <1% recurrence, <1% hydrocele formation, and <0.5% testicular atrophy (Goldstein et al., J Urol 2020).

### commonConcerns

| concern | response |
|---------|----------|
| Will it hurt? | You will have anaesthesia during the procedure, so you will feel nothing. Afterwards, most men describe the discomfort as a dull ache — similar to being lightly kicked. This is well controlled with regular paracetamol and ibuprofen for the first 3–5 days. Severe pain is unusual and should be reported. |
| Will there be a visible scar? | The incision is approximately 2–3 cm long, placed in the natural groin crease (subinguinal approach). It heals well and becomes barely visible within a few months. Dissolvable sutures are used — you will not need stitch removal. |
| Can the varicocele come back? | With the microsurgical technique, the recurrence rate is less than 1%. This is significantly lower than open or laparoscopic approaches because the operating microscope allows precise identification of every abnormal vein. |
| When can I have sex again? | Most men can resume sexual activity comfortably from 2 weeks onwards. There is no restriction on ejaculation from a wound-healing perspective — the concern is simply comfort. Resume when it feels right. |
| Will it improve my fertility? | If the varicocele was causing impaired semen quality, improvement is expected in 60–70% of men within 3–6 months. Your fertility team will arrange a repeat semen analysis at 3 months. Improvement continues for up to 12 months. |
| Will it affect my testosterone? | Varicocele repair has been shown to improve testosterone levels in men with pre-operative testosterone deficiency. If your testosterone was normal before surgery, it is unlikely to change significantly. |
| What if I develop a lump or swelling afterwards? | Some scrotal swelling is expected in the first 1–2 weeks. A new, persistent, painless fluid collection (hydrocele) occurs in less than 1% of microsurgical repairs. If you notice progressive swelling beyond 2 weeks, contact the practice. |
| Can I fly after surgery? | Short-haul flights (under 4 hours) are generally fine from 7 days post-operatively. For long-haul flights, wait 2 weeks. Stay hydrated and walk regularly during the flight. |

**normalExperiences**

You have had a microsurgical varicocele correction today. The procedure was performed through a small incision in your groin crease using an operating microscope.

You may notice:
- A dull, aching discomfort in the groin and scrotum — this is expected and well controlled with your prescribed pain relief
- Mild scrotal swelling, which may feel like a sense of heaviness
- Slight drowsiness or nausea from the anaesthesia, which typically clears within a few hours
- A small dressing over the wound site in your groin crease
- Numbness around the incision — this is from the local anaesthetic injected during surgery and wears off within 6–12 hours

**forecast**

The first 48 hours are usually the most uncomfortable. Swelling often peaks around day 2–3, then steadily improves. By day 5, most men describe the discomfort as mild background aching rather than sharp pain.

**redFlags**

- Severe pain not controlled by regular paracetamol and ibuprofen
- Heavy bleeding soaking through the dressing
- Scrotal swelling that is rapidly increasing in size over hours
- Difficulty passing urine
- Fever above 38°C
- Nausea or vomiting that prevents you from keeping down fluids or medication

| ShowerHead | Shower | avoid |
| Car | Driving | avoid |

| Heart | Sexual activity | avoid |

**nurseNote**

Wear your supportive underwear from the moment you get dressed after surgery. It makes a real difference to comfort — think of it as a compression bandage for the scrotum. Place a folded flannel between the ice pack and your skin to prevent cold burns. Set a phone alarm for your paracetamol doses so you stay ahead of the pain rather than chasing it.

**nurseName**

Caroline

**whyThisHappens**

The aching you feel is from the surgical handling of the spermatic cord — the bundle of blood vessels, nerves, and the vas deferens that runs from the testicle up through the groin. During the procedure, the abnormal veins within this cord were identified under the microscope and individually ligated (tied off), while the testicular artery, lymphatic vessels, and vas deferens were carefully preserved.

Swelling occurs because the body sends extra fluid to the area as part of the natural inflammatory healing response. This is your immune system doing its job — bringing repair cells to the surgical site. The scrotal support and ice work together to limit this swelling by providing external compression and reducing blood flow to the surface tissues.

### Day 1 — First Morning

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **dayNumber** | 1 |
| **phase** | early |
| **phaseLabel** | First 48 Hours |
| **title** | First Morning |

**normalExperiences**

You may notice that scrotal swelling and bruising appear worse this morning than they did last night. This is expected — gravity causes fluid and blood to pool in the dependent tissues overnight.

You may also notice:
- Bruising spreading from the groin towards the inner thigh or even the base of the penis — this looks alarming but is entirely expected with scrotal surgery
- The scrotum feeling heavier or more swollen than yesterday
- Stiffness in the groin when standing up from lying down
- Mild stinging when passing urine for the first time today — this is from the catheter if one was used briefly during anaesthesia, and resolves within 24 hours

**forecast**

Swelling typically peaks today or tomorrow, then begins to improve. Bruising changes colour over the coming week — from dark purple to blue-green to yellow — before fading completely. Each colour change means your body is breaking down and reabsorbing the bruise.

**redFlags**

- Rapidly increasing scrotal swelling (noticeably larger over a few hours)
- Fever above 38°C
- Redness, warmth, or discharge from the wound
- Inability to pass urine
- Pain that is worsening despite regular medication

**activities**

| icon | label | status |
|------|-------|--------|
| Bed | Rest at home, move to the sofa | allowed |
| Footprints | Short walks to the kitchen and bathroom | allowed |
| Pill | Continue paracetamol every 6 hours | allowed |
| IceCube | Continue ice packs: 20 on / 20 off | allowed |
| ShowerHead | Shower (keep wound dry with cling film if needed) | caution |
| Car | Driving | avoid |
| Briefcase | Work | avoid |
| Dumbbell | Exercise | avoid |

**nurseNote**

If you slept on your back with a pillow under your knees, you probably had a more comfortable night. Try to keep doing this for the next few days. When you get up, roll onto your side first and push up with your arms rather than using your abdominal muscles — this avoids pulling on the groin wound. If your bruising looks dramatic, take a photo to compare tomorrow. You will be surprised how quickly it changes.

**nurseName**

Sarah

**whyThisHappens**

Bruising tracks downward because of gravity. The blood that leaked from tiny vessels during surgery follows the tissue planes — the natural channels between layers of fat and muscle — and settles in the lowest point. The scrotum, inner thigh, and perineum are common destinations. This is not a sign that anything has gone wrong. It is simply physics.

The swelling peaks at 24–48 hours because the acute inflammatory response is at its most active during this window. Inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, histamine, cytokines) cause blood vessels to become more permeable, allowing fluid to enter the tissues. This is the same process that causes a sprained ankle to swell — protective, temporary, and self-limiting.

### Day 2 — Peak Swelling

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **dayNumber** | 2 |
| **phase** | early |
| **phaseLabel** | First 48 Hours |
| **title** | Peak Swelling |

**normalExperiences**

Today is often the day swelling reaches its maximum. You may notice:
- The scrotum looks and feels notably larger than your baseline — some men describe it as feeling like a small grapefruit on the affected side
- Bruising has spread and darkened
- Walking feels more comfortable than yesterday, but you still move cautiously
- The dull ache is present but manageable with medication

**forecast**

From tomorrow, the trajectory changes. Swelling begins to reduce day by day. Most men notice a meaningful improvement by day 5. The ice packs become less necessary after today — your body's own healing mechanisms take over from here.

**redFlags**

- Fever above 38°C
- Severe pain not relieved by medication
- Heavy bleeding or large clots
- Sudden calf swelling or chest pain (rare but important — signs of deep vein thrombosis)

**activities**

| icon | label | status |
|------|-------|--------|
| Footprints | Walk around the house regularly (every 1–2 hours) | allowed |
| ShowerHead | Gentle shower — let warm water run over the wound, pat dry | allowed |
| Sofa | Alternate between resting and gentle movement | allowed |
| Car | Driving | avoid |
| ShoppingBag | Lifting anything heavier than a mug | avoid |
| Dumbbell | Exercise | avoid |

**nurseNote**

You can shower today. Let warm water run over the wound — do not scrub it or use any products directly on it. Pat dry with a clean towel. If the adhesive strips (Steri-Strips) over the wound are still in place, leave them — they will peel off on their own over the next 7–10 days. If they get wet, that is fine. After your shower, put on fresh supportive underwear.

**nurseName**

Michael

**whyThisHappens**

Day 2 is the peak because the body's inflammatory cascade follows a predictable timeline. The initial injury signal (from surgery) triggers a wave of neutrophils (first-responder immune cells) that peaks at 24–48 hours. These cells clean up damaged tissue and release signals that recruit the next wave of repair cells (macrophages). As the neutrophil activity subsides, so does the swelling. Think of it as the cleanup crew finishing the most intense phase of their work.

**normalExperiences**

- Worsening pain after a period of improvement
- New redness or warmth around the wound
- Discharge (yellow, green, or foul-smelling) from the wound

The wound should be sealing well. You may notice:

- Persistent fever

One week since surgery. You may notice:

- Fever above 38°C

**whyThisHappens**

At one week, the wound has progressed from the inflammatory phase into the proliferative phase of healing. Fibroblasts (repair cells) are actively depositing collagen — the structural protein that gives the wound its strength. At this stage, the wound has approximately 10–15% of its final strength. This is why it looks healed but is still vulnerable to stress. By 3 weeks it reaches about 20%, and by 6 weeks approximately 50–60%. Full maturation takes 6–12 months, though functional strength is adequate well before then.

**whyThisHappens**

At 3 weeks, the wound has entered the remodelling phase of healing. The collagen fibres laid down during the proliferative phase are now being reorganised and cross-linked — like a scaffolding being gradually replaced by permanent structure. The wound currently has approximately 20% of its final tensile strength, which is adequate for all normal activities. It will continue to strengthen over the next 6 months, reaching approximately 80% of unwounded tissue strength. The scar remodels from raised and pink to flat and pale over this period.

The varicocele itself — the dilated veins that were ligated — will gradually thrombose (clot) and be reabsorbed by the body over the coming weeks. Blood that previously flowed through these abnormal veins is now rerouted through healthy collateral vessels. If the varicocele was causing impaired testicular function (reduced sperm production or testosterone), improvement begins from the point these veins are no longer draining blood away from the testicle. This is why semen parameter improvement takes 3–6 months — one full cycle of sperm production (spermatogenesis) takes approximately 72 days.
