## Procedure-Level Fields (`ggoProcedure`)

### preOperativeInstructions

You will have this procedure under local or general anaesthesia, as a day case. If general anaesthesia is planned, follow your fasting instructions: no food for 6 hours and clear water only up to 2 hours before your scheduled arrival time. If local anaesthesia is planned, you may eat and drink normally beforehand unless told otherwise.

Stop any blood-thinning medication (aspirin, ibuprofen, warfarin, rivaroxaban) only if your surgeon has specifically told you to do so — do not stop these without instruction.

On the morning of surgery:
- Shower using your normal soap. There is no need for special antiseptic wash unless instructed.
- Wear loose, comfortable clothing — preferably tracksuit bottoms or similar. Avoid tight jeans or restrictive underwear for the journey home.
- Bring supportive underwear (snug briefs, not boxers) to wear after surgery. This holds the dressing in place and reduces swelling.
- Arrange for a responsible adult to collect you if you are having general anaesthesia. You will not be able to drive yourself home.
- If having local anaesthesia only, you may drive yourself home if you feel comfortable, but having someone with you is advisable.
- Leave jewellery and valuables at home.

You will typically arrive 1–2 hours before your procedure time. Your anaesthetist (if applicable) and Mr Ollandini will see you beforehand to answer any remaining questions and mark the surgical site.

### postOperativeInstructions

You will go home the same day. If you had general anaesthesia, you will be discharged once you have passed urine, eaten a light meal, and your pain is comfortable with oral medication.

Key points for the first 6 weeks:
- Wear supportive underwear (snug briefs) continuously for the first 2 weeks, including at night. This holds the penis against your body, reduces swelling, and prevents the wound catching on clothing.
- Take paracetamol (1g) every 6 hours regularly for the first 3–5 days, as prescribed by your surgeon. Ibuprofen (400mg) can be added if needed and not contraindicated — always take with food.
- Keep the wound dry for 48 hours. After that, shower normally and let warm water run over the area — do not scrub or use soap directly on the wound. Pat dry gently.
- Do not take baths, swim, or use saunas for 2 weeks.
- Avoid lifting anything heavier than a full kettle for 2 weeks. No gym, running, or contact sports for 3 weeks.
- You may return to desk-based work after 7–10 days. Physical or manual work requires 2–3 weeks off.
- Sexual activity: avoid for a minimum of 6 weeks. Resume only after surgeon clearance.
- Erections during healing are expected and will not damage the wound, though they may cause temporary discomfort. They do not require treatment.

Your follow-up appointment is typically at 6 weeks. If you have any concerns before then, contact the practice.

### expectedOutcomes

Swelling and bruising of the penis (penile shaft) and the foreskin stump area are expected and typically peak at day 2–3. Penile oedema (swelling caused by fluid accumulation) can look dramatic — some men describe the penis as appearing significantly larger than usual. This resolves over 2–3 weeks.

The glans (head of the penis) will feel intensely sensitive once exposed. This is because the foreskin (prepuce) has been protecting the glans for your entire life. The sensitivity reduces progressively over weeks to months as the glans undergoes keratinisation — the development of a slightly thicker protective outer layer. Most men report that sensitivity reaches a comfortable baseline within 4–8 weeks.

Sutures are absorbable and dissolve within 10–14 days. You do not need stitch removal. The suture line (circumferential wound) will initially appear raised and pink, then flatten and fade over 3–6 months.

The final cosmetic appearance continues to improve for 6–12 months. The circumcision scar typically settles as a thin line around the shaft, the colour and prominence of which depends on your skin tone and healing characteristics.

Complication rates for adult circumcision performed by a specialist are low: bleeding requiring intervention occurs in approximately 1–2%, wound infection in 2–4%, and unsatisfactory cosmetic outcome requiring revision in approximately 1–3% (BAUS PIL 2023).

**normalExperiences**

You have had an adult circumcision today. The foreskin (prepuce) has been removed using a sleeve technique, and the wound has been closed with absorbable sutures. A dressing has been applied around the circumcision line.

You may notice:
- A dull, stinging discomfort around the wound — this is expected and well controlled with your prescribed pain relief
- Swelling of the penile shaft, particularly around the suture line and the foreskin stump area
- Mild oozing or spotting of blood on the dressing — a small amount is expected
- Numbness of the penile skin from the local anaesthetic injected during surgery, which wears off within 4–8 hours
- The glans feeling exposed and sensitive when the local anaesthetic wears off — this is your first experience of the glans without foreskin coverage

**forecast**

The first 48 hours are usually the most uncomfortable. Swelling peaks around day 2–3, then steadily improves. The intense glans sensitivity reduces noticeably within the first week. By day 5, most men describe the discomfort as background awareness rather than active pain.

**redFlags**

- Heavy bleeding soaking through the dressing (more than light spotting)
- Severe pain not controlled by regular paracetamol and ibuprofen
- Difficulty passing urine
- Fever above 38°C
- Nausea or vomiting that prevents you from keeping down fluids or medication
- The dressing becoming excessively tight due to swelling — if this happens, contact the practice for advice on dressing adjustment

**nurseNote**

Wear your supportive briefs from the moment you get dressed after surgery. They serve two purposes: holding the dressing in place and keeping the penis against your body to reduce swelling. Loose boxers will not provide this support. Place a folded flannel between the ice pack and your underwear — never apply ice directly to the skin. Set a phone alarm for your paracetamol doses so you stay ahead of the pain rather than chasing it. When you lie down, rest on your back — lying on your front puts pressure on the wound.

**nurseName**

Caroline

**whyThisHappens**

The swelling you see is your body's acute inflammatory response to surgery. When the foreskin is removed and the wound edges are sutured together, the body sends a cascade of inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, histamine, cytokines) to the area. These chemicals make the local blood vessels more permeable, allowing fluid, white blood cells, and healing proteins to flood into the tissue. This is the same process that causes any wound to swell — it is protective and essential for healing.

The penile shaft swells more dramatically than many surgical sites because the skin of the penis is loosely attached and has significant capacity to expand with fluid. The foreskin remnant (the inner mucosal surface that remains) is particularly prone to oedema because it is thin, vascular tissue. This is why penile circumcision swelling can look out of proportion to the size of the procedure — the anatomy amplifies the visual effect.

### Day 1 — First Morning

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **dayNumber** | 1 |
| **phase** | early |
| **phaseLabel** | First 48 Hours |
| **title** | First Morning |

**normalExperiences**

You may notice that swelling and bruising appear worse this morning than last night. This is expected — gravity and the overnight inflammatory response cause fluid to accumulate in the penile tissues.

You may also notice:
- Penile oedema (fluid swelling) that makes the penis appear significantly larger or puffier than usual — particularly the area just behind the glans and the foreskin stump
- Bruising spreading along the shaft or towards the base of the penis — this looks concerning but is expected
- The glans feeling intensely sensitive to any contact — underwear, bedsheets, air
- Mild stinging when passing urine, particularly at the tip — urine running over the suture line causes this
- The dressing may be stained with dried blood or yellowish fluid (serous exudate) — this is expected wound drainage

**forecast**

Swelling typically peaks today or tomorrow. The penile oedema can look dramatic — some men describe it as alarming. This is the expected peak of the inflammatory response and it begins to resolve from day 3 onwards. The glans sensitivity, while intense now, reduces noticeably with each passing day as the exposed surface begins to adapt.

**redFlags**

- Heavy bleeding (fresh, bright red blood actively dripping or soaking through dressing)
- Fever above 38°C
- Green, yellow, or foul-smelling discharge from the wound
- Difficulty passing urine or inability to pass urine
- Pain that is worsening despite regular medication
- The dressing becoming so tight it feels constricting

| Footprints | Short walks to the kitchen and bathroom | allowed |

| IceCube | Continue ice packs: 20 on / 20 off (through underwear) | allowed |
| ShowerHead | Shower (keep dressing dry) | caution |
| Car | Driving | avoid |
| Briefcase | Work | avoid |

**nurseNote**

If the glans sensitivity is particularly bothersome, try applying a thin layer of petroleum jelly (Vaseline) to the glans before putting on your briefs. This creates a barrier that reduces friction. If your dressing is soaked through with blood, contact the practice. If it is stained but not actively bleeding, this is expected — leave it in place.

Sarah

**whyThisHappens**

The swelling peaks at 24–48 hours because the acute inflammatory cascade is at its most active during this window. Neutrophils (first-responder immune cells) flood the surgical site, peaking at this time. The penile tissue is particularly prone to visible oedema because the dartos fascia — the thin muscular layer beneath the penile skin — creates a potential space that fills readily with inflammatory fluid.

The oedema often looks worst in the dependent (lowest) part: for men lying down, this means the foreskin remnant area swells prominently; for men who are upright, gravity pulls fluid towards the glans. This gravitational effect is why the swelling can shift in appearance throughout the day depending on your position.

### Day 2 — Peak Swelling

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **dayNumber** | 2 |
| **phase** | early |
| **phaseLabel** | First 48 Hours |
| **title** | Peak Swelling |

**normalExperiences**

Today is often the day swelling reaches its maximum. You may notice:
- The penile shaft appears swollen and discoloured — purple, dusky, or mottled. This is expected vascular congestion combined with oedema
- The area just behind the glans (the sulcus) may be particularly puffy, with the inner foreskin remnant ballooning outward
- Bruising has spread and darkened
- The glans remains sensitive but you may find that contact with underwear is becoming more tolerable than yesterday
- Walking feels more comfortable than yesterday, though you still move cautiously

**forecast**

From tomorrow, the trajectory changes. Swelling begins to reduce day by day. Most men notice a meaningful improvement by day 5. The ice packs become less necessary after today — your body's own healing mechanisms take over from here. If your surgeon has advised you to begin gentle hygiene of the wound, this can start today or tomorrow.

**redFlags**

- Fever above 38°C
- Severe pain not relieved by medication
- Heavy bleeding or expanding haematoma (a firm, dark swelling under the skin that is growing)
- Green or foul-smelling discharge from the suture line
- Inability to pass urine

**activities**

| icon | label | status |
|------|-------|--------|
| Footprints | Walk around the house regularly (every 1–2 hours) | allowed |
| ShowerHead | Gentle shower — let warm water run over the wound if dressing has been removed, pat dry | allowed |
| Sofa | Alternate between resting and gentle movement | allowed |
| Car | Driving | avoid |

| Dumbbell | Exercise | avoid |

**nurseNote**

If your dressing has been instructed to come off today, do so in the shower. Let warm water soften the dressing before gently peeling it away. If it sticks, soak it further — do not pull it off dry. Once the dressing is removed, let the area air-dry or pat gently with a clean towel. You do not need to replace the dressing unless advised. After washing, the wound should be left open to air when possible. Put on clean supportive briefs afterwards. If the Steri-Strips are still in place on the suture line, leave them — they will peel off on their own.

**nurseName**

Michael

**whyThisHappens**

Day 2 is the peak because the body's inflammatory cascade follows a predictable timeline. The initial injury signal (from surgery) triggers a wave of neutrophils that peaks at 24–48 hours. These cells clean up damaged tissue and release signals that recruit the next wave of repair cells (macrophages). As the neutrophil activity subsides, so does the swelling.

The dramatic appearance of circumcision swelling is partly anatomical. The penile skin has no subcutaneous fat layer — it sits directly over the dartos fascia and Buck's fascia. Fluid that accumulates in these layers has nowhere to hide and is immediately visible on the surface. This is why a relatively small volume of inflammatory fluid creates a disproportionately swollen appearance. The good news: because the tissue is thin and well-vascularised, it also reabsorbs fluid efficiently once the inflammatory peak passes.

**whyThisHappens**

The intense glans sensitivity you are experiencing is a direct consequence of removing the foreskin. The foreskin is not just a covering — it maintains a warm, moist, protected environment over the glans. The glans surface in an uncircumcised man is a mucosal membrane, similar to the inside of your lip. It has a high density of free nerve endings and is accustomed to being shielded from direct contact.

When the foreskin is removed, the glans is suddenly exposed to air, fabric, and friction for the first time. The nerve endings that were previously protected now fire in response to stimuli they have never encountered. This is not damage — it is the sensory system adjusting to a new environment.

Over the coming weeks and months, the glans undergoes keratinisation: the outer layer of cells gradually thickens and becomes drier, forming a mild protective barrier similar to the skin on the rest of your body. This process reduces the sensitivity to a comfortable level. It does not eliminate sensation — it calibrates it.

**nurseName**

Michael

**whyThisHappens**

At one week, the wound has progressed from the inflammatory phase into the proliferative phase of healing. Fibroblasts (repair cells) are actively depositing collagen — the structural protein that gives the wound its strength. At this stage, the wound has approximately 10–15% of its final tensile strength. This is why it looks healed on the surface but remains vulnerable to mechanical stress.

The sutures are dissolving through a process called hydrolysis — water molecules gradually break down the polymer chains that make up the suture material. Absorbable sutures used in circumcision (typically polyglactin 910 / Vicryl Rapide) are designed to lose their tensile strength within 10–14 days and be fully absorbed within 42 days. By day 7, the sutures have done the majority of their work in holding the wound edges together. The wound is now generating its own collagen scaffolding to take over from the sutures.

**whyThisHappens**

At two weeks, the wound is in the mid-proliferative phase of healing. Fibroblasts have been laying down type III collagen — a provisional, somewhat disorganised scaffolding — since the first week. This collagen provides the structural basis for wound integrity. At this stage, the wound has approximately 20–25% of its final tensile strength.

The process now shifts towards collagen remodelling: the initial type III collagen is gradually replaced by type I collagen (stronger, more organised). This remodelling phase continues for months. By 6 weeks, the wound reaches approximately 50–60% of its final strength. By 3 months, approximately 80%. Full maturation — reaching the maximum strength the scar will ever achieve (approximately 80% of unwounded tissue) — takes 6–12 months.

This timeline explains why the 6-week sexual activity restriction exists. Even though the wound looks healed at 2 weeks, the internal tensile strength is insufficient for the mechanical stress of sexual activity. By 6 weeks, the wound has enough collagen cross-linking to withstand normal use.

**whyThisHappens**

At 6 weeks, the wound has entered the remodelling phase of healing. The collagen fibres laid down during the proliferative phase are being reorganised and cross-linked — the provisional scaffolding is gradually replaced by permanent structure. The wound currently has approximately 50–60% of its final tensile strength, which is adequate for all normal activities including sexual activity.

The scar will continue to remodel for 6–12 months, reaching approximately 80% of unwounded tissue strength. The scar transitions from raised and pink to flat and pale during this period — this is most noticeable in the 3–6 month window.

The glans has undergone the early stages of keratinisation by this point. The outermost layer of the glans epithelium (the surface cells) has begun to develop a stratum corneum — a slightly thickened, drier protective layer. This is the same process that differentiates the skin on the back of your hand (keratinised, dry, relatively tough) from the inside of your cheek (mucosal, moist, sensitive). The glans does not become as keratinised as external skin — it reaches an intermediate state that provides protection from friction while preserving erogenous sensitivity.

Long-term adaptation of sensation is a neuroplastic process as well as a structural one. The sensory nerve endings in the glans do not disappear — the brain recalibrates its interpretation of the signals they send. This is why most men report that sexual sensation, while subjectively different after circumcision, is not reduced in quality. The brain adapts to the new sensory input over weeks to months.
